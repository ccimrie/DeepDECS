DeepDECS

Framework for safer controllers

Autonomous systems have been utilising deep neural networks (DNNs) to perceive the world around them. However, no DNN is ever perfect, which can be devastating in safety-critical applications. DeepDECS is a developed solution able to capture the uncertainty of DNN perception components through the use of robustness verification methods From this DeepDECS produces safe autonomous system controllers for such applications.

The diagram on the right is the DeepDECS framework, and as can be seen DeepDECS requires four components from the user. First a discrete time Markov Chain (DTMC) of the application the autonomous system is to operate within, and second a requirements file defined via probabilistic computation tree logic (PCTL) notation. It is important to note that the DTMC provided will assume that the perception of the system is perfect. Lastly a pre trained perception DNN to be utilised in the autonomous system, and along an accompanying representative test dataset. These last two components are utilised, along with \(n\) verification methods, in step 1 of DeepDECS to derive a set of $2^{n}$ confusion matrices. The accuracy derived from the confusion matrices will be the probabilities for our generated augmented DTMC, step 2 of DeepDECS. Our augmented model now contains an accurate representation of the imperfect perception unit. Step 3 of DeepDECS is controller synthesis, achieved via formal methods with the augmented DTMC and PCTL requirements.

DeepDECS Tutorial

How fast to drive on potential ice?

Say you have a autonomous car driving down a road and the car is equipped with a perception unit (a DNN). The aim of the perception unit is to determine if the next segment of road is icy or not. Having this information is invaluable as the car should not drive fast on icy roads, but the perception unit is not perfect so how do we best ascertain a safety critical controller? Case Studies

Mobile robot collision avoidance & Shared autonomy for driving

To evaluate DeepDECS two case studies were studied. The first is a robot in a warehouse where it must travel from one point to another in a known map. The robot navigates by traversing waypoints, and finds the shortest route using Dijkstra's algorithm. The robot, however, is not the only agent operating in the warehouse, and now the robot must also minimise dangerous collisions. At each waypoint the robot first detects if a collider is present, and if so uses a DNN to predict whether a collision will occur if the robot proceeds. The video on the left shows the robot utilising a safe controller synthesised from the DeepDECS framework.

The second case study focused on shared control in autonomous driving, see the image on the left. Given the complexity there will be times while driving that the autonomous car will not be able to operate, thus transferring control over to the human driver. This therefore requires the human driver to be alert at all times should a transfer of control is required. The car predicts the human driver's attentiveness using a DNN, and needs to decide which alerts to use if the human driver is not paying attention, such as lights, vibration and sound. For this case study real data was used of human drivers in a simulated scenario.

For more detail on the case studies you can read the paper. Access to the code and models can be found on this repo.
